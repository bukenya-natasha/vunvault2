Password Hygiene for Teams That Hate Changing Passwords

How to roll out a password manager and MFA without an IT department revolt — informed by the latest 2025 and 2026 global research, written for people who actually have to make this work.

VUNVAULT Team ~18 minute read Updated 2026

The Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About

Picture a Monday morning. A member of the accounts team opens her laptop and encounters the familiar message: "Your password has expired. Please create a new one." She sighs, types "Summer2026!", gets rejected for being too similar to her previous password, and after three more failed attempts and ten wasted minutes, finally gets in — only to immediately write the new password on a sticky note tucked under her keyboard. Security policy satisfied, security reality unchanged.

This is not a rare scenario. It is the global norm. A study published in 2026 analysing 19 billion passwords leaked between 2024 and 2025 found that only 6% were unique. The remaining 94% were reused or duplicated across multiple accounts. Meanwhile, the Verizon 2025 Data Breach Investigations Report confirmed that compromised credentials remain the leading initial access vector in breaches for the second consecutive year, present in 22% of all confirmed incidents and in 88% of basic web application attacks.

The financial toll is equally sobering. The IBM Cost of a Data Breach 2025 Report places the average cost of a breach where stolen credentials were the entry point at US$4.67 million, with a mean time to identify and contain of 246 days. By the time most organisations discover they have been compromised through a stolen password, the attacker has been inside for the better part of a year.

And yet the average person in 2026 manages roughly 250 passwords — a number that has doubled in five years. Seventy percent of adults report feeling overwhelmed by the sheer volume of credentials they are expected to maintain. The security measures designed to protect them are instead driving them toward the very behaviours that make everything less secure.

Your team is not the problem. Friction is the problem.

This is the password paradox: the more organisations tighten their password policies, the more creatively people find ways around them. The good news is that 2025 and 2026 research from around the world now points clearly toward what actually works — and it turns out that better security, done correctly, requires less effort from users, not more.

Why Traditional Password Policies Are Failing — The Science

For decades, the standard advice has been consistent: change passwords every 90 days, use complex combinations of letters, numbers and symbols, never reuse them, and make them as difficult to guess as possible. The result of rigidly following this advice is a global workforce full of P@ssw0rd1 variations and sticky notes.

A landmark 2026 study from the University at Albany's Massry School of Business, published in the European Journal of Information Systems, gave a name to what most people already know intuitively: security fatigue. This is a state of mental exhaustion that arises when security requirements repeatedly interfere with a person's primary work duties and exceed their capacity for sustained self-regulation. When people are fatigued, they do not comply more reluctantly — they simply stop complying.

The problem extends well beyond frontline employees. Proofpoint's 2025 Voice of the CISO Report found that 63% of Chief Information Security Officers had experienced or witnessed burnout in the previous year. A separate vendor-agnostic survey of 5,000 IT and cybersecurity professionals across 17 countries, conducted in early 2025, put the figure even higher, with 76% reporting cyber fatigue or burnout on a frequent or constant basis — and 69% reporting that the problem had worsened between 2023 and 2024.

This fatigue has measurable business consequences. Research consistently links higher security fatigue to lower compliance, lower productivity, and increased error rates. On the customer-facing side, 87% of US consumers have abandoned a purchase or sign-up process because of login difficulties, and people who find authentication procedures too cumbersome are 46% more likely to abandon a transaction entirely.

The data on complexity requirements is equally uncomfortable. Analysis of breached credentials shows that only 3% of compromised passwords met basic complexity requirements — meaning the remaining 97% were already "simple" by policy standards. Complexity requirements, in practice, produce passwords that are hard for humans to remember and often easy for computers to crack, since most people follow predictable patterns when constructing them.

The Modern Solution, Part One: Password Managers

A password manager is, in the most straightforward terms, a digital vault. It remembers all your passwords so that you do not have to. Instead of maintaining 250 different credentials across your memory, sticky notes, and spreadsheets, you remember a single master password — or increasingly, use a fingerprint or face scan — and the manager handles everything else. It generates passwords that are genuinely random and unique, fills them in automatically, and stores them in encrypted form that even the software company itself cannot access.

The business case is not theoretical. Research spanning 2024 and 2025 found that individuals who used password managers experienced identity or credential theft at a rate of 17%, compared with 32% for those who did not — meaning people without a password manager face nearly double the risk of having their credentials stolen.

The market reflects growing recognition of this: the global enterprise password management market was valued at US$3.2 billion in 2026 and is projected to reach US$9.4 billion by 2033. The fastest growth is in the Asia-Pacific region, which is posting a compound annual growth rate of 24.13% between 2026 and 2031.

Where adoption stands globally

Despite the clear evidence, adoption remains far below where it should be. Globally, approximately 30% of internet users use a password manager of any kind, including browser-built-in tools. Only 15% use a dedicated, standalone manager. In the United States — one of the more mature markets — 36% of adults are subscribed to password manager services, up from 34% the prior year. Yet 65% of US respondents in one major survey reported that they do not trust password managers.

That trust gap is the most important data point for anyone planning a rollout. Resistance is rarely a matter of principle — it is almost always a matter of experience, familiarity, and how the tool was introduced. Among people who do not currently use a password manager, research consistently finds that more than 75% are open to trying one when it is usable, secure, and properly explained. The barrier is not the technology itself but the way it is presented and supported.

Organisationally, only around 25% of companies currently require employees to use a password manager. That means simply having a clear, supported mandate already positions an organisation ahead of three quarters of its peers.

The Modern Solution, Part Two: Multi-Factor Authentication

Multi-factor authentication, commonly known as MFA, requires a user to provide more than one form of verification before accessing an account. The most familiar form is a code sent to your phone, but the category has expanded dramatically to include push notifications, hardware security keys, and biometric verification through fingerprint or face recognition.

The evidence for MFA is among the strongest in security research. According to Microsoft's Digital Defense Report 2025, phishing-resistant MFA blocks more than 99% of identity-based attacks, even in cases where the attacker already holds a valid username and password. Identity-based attacks surged 32% in the first half of 2025 alone. Despite this, Microsoft estimates that 99.9% of compromised accounts in their analysis did not have MFA enabled.

The global adoption gap

Workforce MFA adoption reached 70% globally as of January 2025, according to the Okta Secure Sign-in Trends Report. That figure sounds encouraging until broken down by organisation size. Among enterprises with more than 10,000 employees, approximately 87% use MFA. Among small and medium-sized businesses, the adoption rate falls to between 27% and 45%, depending on the market. Perhaps most striking: nearly two-thirds of global SMBs do not use MFA and, as of the most recent surveys, have no plans to implement it in the near future.

Regionally, the Asia-Pacific market is currently growing the fastest, with MFA adoption climbing seven percentage points year over year driven by acceleration in Hong Kong, South Korea, and Japan. North America and Europe each grew by two percentage points over the same period.

The threat that makes MFA non-negotiable

Microsoft blocked roughly 7,000 password attacks per second across its identity platform in 2025. Credential stuffing — where attackers use stolen username and password pairs from previous breaches — accounted for 19% of all authentication attempts on a median daily basis across single sign-on provider logs. Against this volume, a password alone is not a defence.

The push bombing problem — and the fix

As MFA adoption has grown, attackers have adapted. Push bombing — where an attacker repeatedly sends MFA approval requests to a victim's phone hoping they will tap "approve" just to make the notifications stop — has become a significant and growing attack vector. The recommended defence, now adopted by default across Microsoft, Okta, and most enterprise identity providers, is number matching: the app displays a number on screen and requires the user to select the same number in the authenticator before approving. This single change eliminates the push bombing attack vector almost entirely.

A further evolution is the shift toward adaptive authentication — systems that assess risk continuously throughout a session rather than just at login. Research from the Verizon 2025 DBIR identified a growing share of breaches involving stolen session tokens or post-authentication abuse, meaning that a single verification gate at login is no longer sufficient for high-risk environments. Conditional access policies tied to device health, location, and behaviour patterns are now the higher-assurance standard.

The Revolution Already Underway: Passkeys

If there is one development in authentication security that deserves to be called a genuine breakthrough rather than an incremental improvement, it is passkeys. On World Passkey Day 2026, the FIDO Alliance — the industry body that maintains the open standards behind the technology — announced that an estimated five billion passkeys are now in active use worldwide. That milestone, based on research spanning 11,000 consumers and 1,400 enterprise decision-makers across ten countries including the US, UK, France, Germany, Australia, Singapore, Japan, South Korea, China, and India, marks the moment that passwordless authentication crossed from early adopter territory into genuine mainstream use.

A passkey is a cryptographic credential — a form of authentication built on open FIDO2 standards developed jointly by the FIDO Alliance and the World Wide Web Consortium. When you sign in with a passkey, your device uses a private key stored in its secure hardware enclave to prove your identity. The private key never leaves your device. There is nothing to type, nothing to remember, and nothing for an attacker to intercept or phish — because there is no password in the exchange at all.

What the numbers look like in practice

Consumer awareness of passkeys has reached 90%, up from 75% in the previous year's survey. Three quarters of consumers have enabled a passkey on at least one account. And 68% of organisations worldwide have either deployed passkeys for employee authentication or are actively in the process of doing so. The speed advantage is concrete and measurable: passkey logins average 8.5 seconds compared to 31.2 seconds for traditional MFA — nearly four times faster. Passkeys achieve a 93% login success rate versus 63% for conventional authentication methods.

Real-world deployments are producing proportionate results. Branch Insurance reported a 50% reduction in authentication-related support tickets after integrating passkeys alongside their existing login system. Microsoft reported an 87% reduction in authentication-related costs following a broader shift toward passwordless systems. Across organisations that have deployed passkeys, the average reduction in helpdesk costs directly attributable to authentication sits at 35%.

Andrew Shikiar, Executive Director and CEO of the FIDO Alliance, described the shift plainly: passkeys deliver something the industry has struggled to achieve for decades — authentication that is simultaneously more secure and easier to use. What began as alignment behind open standards has evolved into measurable global adoption across consumer services, enterprises, and critical infrastructure.

The honest caveat

Even with this rapid adoption, passwords remain widely used in parallel. Among organisations that have deployed passkeys, 57% still rely on phishable authentication methods for the majority of daily sign-ins. Deployment is not the finish line — adoption depth is. One third of consumers globally reported an account compromise or breach notification in the past year, rising to 41% in the United States. The infrastructure exists; the consistent use of it does not yet.

Authentication Methods: From Weakest to Strongest

Not all authentication methods offer the same level of protection. The hierarchy below reflects current 2025 and 2026 research on actual attack patterns and real-world breach data, ordered from the method most frequently exploited in attacks to the one most resistant to them.

Avoid
SMS or email one-time codes

Vulnerable to SIM swapping, social engineering of telecom staff, and message interception. Despite this, 41% of users still trust SMS-based authentication, per Yubico's 2025 Global State of Authentication Survey. Increasingly avoided by major regulators and insurers.

Good
Authenticator app codes (TOTP)

Time-based one-time passwords generated by apps such as Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, or Authy. Significantly stronger than SMS and not vulnerable to SIM swapping. Still phishable in sophisticated real-time attacks but effective against the majority of credential theft.

Better
Push notifications with number matching

A push notification is sent to your phone when someone attempts to log in, and you must match a displayed number to approve. Number matching, now enabled by default on Microsoft, Okta, and most major identity platforms, eliminates the push bombing attack vector. CISA endorses this as the recommended interim defence against MFA fatigue attacks.

Best
Passkeys and hardware security keys (FIDO2 / WebAuthn)

Phishing-resistant by design. The private key never leaves the device. No password is transmitted or stored on a server. Hardware keys such as YubiKey add a physical possession requirement that cannot be remotely phished. Phishing-resistant authenticator adoption grew 63% year over year in 2025 per Okta, rising from 8.6% to 14% of users. This is the 2026 gold standard.

The Rollout Strategy: How to Avoid an IT Revolt

Most organisations get this wrong in the same way: they announce a new security policy on a Friday, expect full compliance by Monday, and respond to the inevitable pushback with increasingly punitive enforcement. Given everything the global research shows about fatigue, trust gaps, and the relationship between friction and adoption, there is a better approach.

The most important finding from the FIDO Alliance's 2026 enterprise research is that user resistance, the factor most commonly cited as a concern among organisations that have not yet deployed passkeys or password managers, turns out to be far more manageable than feared. Among organisations that identified user behaviour as a potential barrier, 53% described it as a minor factor requiring some training, not a fundamental obstacle. Only 8% reported it as a primary blocker that directly delayed rollout. The stories organisations tell themselves about what their teams will resist are consistently worse than the reality.

Phase 1
Weeks 1–2
Start with leadership — visibly

Select your password manager and MFA solution. Set up accounts for the executive team and department heads first. Have them use the tools openly and talk about them. When leadership adopts new security tools publicly, the rest of the organisation follows. This is the single most reliable predictor of organisation-wide uptake — more reliable than any policy mandate.

Phase 2
Weeks 3–4
Pilot with informed champions

Identify five to ten technically comfortable employees across different departments. Give them early access, ask for honest feedback, and train them to become informal support for their colleagues. These champions reduce help desk load dramatically during broader rollout and provide credibility that no IT announcement can match.

Phase 3
Month 2
Department-by-department rollout

Roll out to one department at a time, starting with the most digitally fluent. Schedule hands-on training sessions during work hours — a 15-minute live demonstration covering vault creation, browser extension setup, and password generation eliminates most resistance before it forms. Set a migration deadline, create a FAQ from the actual questions people ask, and acknowledge publicly when teams hit their targets.

Phase 4
Month 3
Universal deployment and enforcement

Enable hard enforcement after an adequate enrolment period. Monitor adoption metrics weekly. Centralise enforcement through your identity provider — Okta, Microsoft Entra ID, or Google Workspace — rather than relying on manual compliance checks. Asia-Pacific organisations that followed this centralised model gained seven percentage points of MFA adoption in a single year, demonstrating how much the platform itself can do when configured correctly.

Choosing the Right Tools

The password manager market is expanding rapidly and the leading options are mature, well-audited, and increasingly straightforward to deploy. LastPass holds 23.3% of global market share. 1Password, Dashlane, Keeper, Bitwarden, and RoboForm account for a further significant share, with the remaining market spread across a highly fragmented field of regional and specialist tools. Google and Apple together hold more than 55% of the password manager market through their built-in services, which are a reasonable starting point for individuals but lack the administrative controls that organisations need.

Addressing the Real Objections

"What if the password manager gets hacked?"

It is a fair question and worth answering directly. Modern password managers use zero-knowledge encryption, meaning the software company itself cannot decrypt your data. An attacker who breaches the password manager's servers obtains encrypted vaults that are computationally infeasible to crack without your master password. The comparison is not between "password manager" and "perfect safety" — it is between a password manager and the status quo. The status quo is reused, simple, written-down passwords that are stolen at a rate of 22% of all confirmed breaches. That comparison is not close.

"Our people won't use it."

The data says otherwise, consistently. Among organisations that cited user behaviour as a concern before deploying password managers or passkeys, 53% found it was a minor issue requiring some training. Eight percent found it a serious obstacle. The rest found it was not a significant factor at all. Users do not resist security — they resist friction. Remove the friction through good tooling and proper support, and adoption follows.

"SMS codes are good enough."

They are not, and insurance markets and regulators have started to codify this formally. SIM swapping — where an attacker convinces a telecom operator to transfer a victim's phone number to a new SIM they control — enables complete bypass of SMS-based MFA. Carrier surcharges and regulatory complexity are also making SMS OTP economically unattractive at scale. The direction of travel from CISA, NIST, PCI DSS, and the NIS2 Directive in Europe is uniform: move away from SMS as a primary MFA method.

Regulatory Pressure — Why You Cannot Wait

For organisations that need a business case to take to leadership, the regulatory environment in 2026 provides considerable ammunition. PCI DSS version 4.0.1, the payment card security standard, made MFA mandatory for all access to the cardholder data environment as of March 2025 — extended beyond administrators to every user with any access to payment data, across cloud workloads, workstations, servers, and endpoints. Non-compliance carries penalties of between US$5,000 and US$100,000 per month.

The EU's NIS2 Directive now requires multi-factor or continuous authentication for essential and important entities under Article 21. Microsoft mandates MFA for all Microsoft 365 administrative centre access as of February 2026. The FTC Safeguards Rule requires MFA for any employee account with access to customer data. CMMC 2.0, covering US defence contractors, became enforceable in November 2025 with strong authentication requirements.

The insurance market is enforcing the same standards indirectly: cyber-insurance policies are being refused or significantly repriced for organisations that rely on email-only or SMS OTP authentication. Underwriters have begun treating phishing-resistant MFA not as a best practice recommendation but as a coverage condition.

Measuring Whether It Is Working

The research linking security fatigue to poor outcomes includes a consistent finding: organisations that track only compliance metrics — the percentage of accounts technically enrolled — consistently overestimate the protection their authentication programme provides. Tracking adoption depth and user experience gives a more accurate picture of actual security posture.

The metrics worth tracking fall into three categories. On adoption: the percentage of employees with active password manager accounts, the percentage of accounts protected by MFA, the passkey enrolment rate, and the ratio of unique to reused passwords in the vault. On security outcomes: failed login attempts, account compromise incidents, mean time to detect a credential compromise, and the volume of credential-related help desk tickets. On user experience: average login time, help desk call volume related to authentication, and employee satisfaction scores on the tools themselves.

On the user experience point, the numbers available from real deployments are genuinely encouraging. Organisations that have moved to passkeys report login time reductions from 31 seconds to 8.5 seconds, helpdesk cost reductions averaging 35%, and in specific cases — such as Branch Insurance — a 50% reduction in authentication-related support tickets. These are not marginal improvements. They are the kind of numbers that make a business case to leadership straightforward.

Building a Security Culture That Lasts

Technology alone cannot solve a problem that is fundamentally about human behaviour. The strongest authentication strategies in the world are undermined by training programmes that treat employees as problems to be managed rather than people to be equipped. And the inverse is equally true: even motivated, security-aware employees cannot compensate for authentication systems that make their working day harder.

Effective security training in 2026 looks different from the annual hour-long compliance presentation. Microlearning, delivered in five-minute modules at the point of relevance, consistently outperforms longer formats. Showing people actual phishing emails from their own industry, rather than generic examples, produces better recognition than abstract explanations. Letting people set up their password manager during a training session — rather than receiving instructions to do it later — closes the gap between learning and action.

On user preferences — 2026 data

When surveyed on which authentication methods they trust most, 53% of respondents rated fingerprint scans as more secure than passwords. 47% rated facial recognition as more secure. Only 6% rated passwords as the most secure method available. When given a choice, 58% of respondents preferred biometrics over passwords. Users are not attached to passwords out of preference — they are stuck with them out of inertia. Change the default, and adoption follows.

The single most important cultural signal is visible leadership behaviour. When an organisation's leadership team openly uses password managers, talks about their own adoption of passkeys, and treats authentication security as an operational priority rather than an IT compliance exercise, the message to the rest of the organisation is unambiguous. Policy alone rarely achieves that.

Start Today — A Practical Action Plan

The evidence from two years of global research on passwords, authentication, and passkeys points to a consistent conclusion: the biggest obstacle to better password security is not technology or cost or even genuine user resistance. It is inertia. Most organisations know what better looks like. Most are simply waiting for a better moment to start.

There is no better moment. Thirty-three percent of consumers globally reported a confirmed account compromise in the past year. In the US that figure is 41%. Against 7,000 password attacks per second, delay is a measured risk decision with a quantifiable cost.

1
Check your own exposure now. Visit haveibeenpwned.com and enter your business email address. If any of your credentials appear in a known breach database, treat those accounts as compromised and change them immediately through a password manager.
2
Choose a password manager this week. Bitwarden is free, open-source, and independently audited — an appropriate starting point for small teams or individuals. 1Password Business or Keeper Enterprise suit organisations with compliance requirements. Do not spend more time evaluating options than deploying one.
3
Enable MFA on every account that supports it. Prioritise email, cloud services, banking, and any system containing customer or member data. Use an authenticator app at minimum. Enable number matching wherever it is available as a default option.
4
Enable a passkey on at least one account this week. Google, Apple, and Microsoft accounts all support passkeys. The experience of logging in with a fingerprint or face scan, rather than a password, is the fastest way to make the case to sceptical colleagues.
5
Present the business case to leadership this month. Lead with the breach cost data — US$4.67 million average, 246-day detection lag — and the compliance obligations relevant to your organisation. Frame the password manager and MFA programme as a cost reduction and operational resilience initiative, not a security overhead.
6
Run a pilot with your champions next month. Identify five to ten people across the organisation who are technically comfortable and willing to be informal advocates. Their honest experience, shared with their colleagues, will do more for adoption than any policy announcement.

The Bottom Line

Password security does not have to be painful. When done correctly, it makes everyone's working life easier, not harder. The global evidence from 2025 and 2026 is unambiguous on this point: five billion passkeys are in active use. Ninety-nine percent of identity attacks are blocked by phishing-resistant authentication. Sixty-eight percent of organisations are deploying passkeys. The tools exist, the evidence is overwhelming, and the user resistance most organisations fear turns out — in practice — to be manageable with decent training and visible support.

The challenge is not technical and it is not principally about budget. It is about replacing a set of deeply ingrained defaults — passwords written on sticky notes, PINs that match birthdays, the same credential used across thirty different systems — with a new set of defaults that are both more secure and genuinely easier to live with.

Make security the path of least resistance. Your team will adopt it — not because they have to, but because it actually works better.

Your team does not hate security. They hate friction, confusion, and feeling like the security measures exist to catch them out rather than protect them. Give them tools that work, support when they need it, and recognition when they do it right — and you will not need to fight for adoption. You will find it already happening.
